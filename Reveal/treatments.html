therapies and treatments

For accurate diagnoses and treatment referrals, please contact a licensed professional.

Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is a common type of talk therapy (psychotherapy) that combines cognitive therapy and behavior therapy. The cognitive aspect of CBT is to help the patient recognize and analyze their thoughts and try to find patterns that might be contributing to their mental state. The behavior aspect follows the theory that human behavior is learned and can therefore be changed through habit modification. In CBT, the patient work with a mental health counselor by attending a srt amount of sessions. CBT is commonly used to treat mental health disorders, such as Depressive Disorders, Anxiety Disorders, and Trauma and Stressor Related Disorders (particularly PTSD); however, it can also be used for Bipolar and Related Disorders, Substance-Related and Addictive Disorders, and Schizophrenia. CBT has a 50-75% effectiveness for overcoming depression and anxiety.

Cognitive Enhancement Therapy (CET)

Cognitive enhancement therapy (CET) is a therapy that is designed for people with schizophrenia and certain types of Bipolar and Related Disorders. In CET, patients learn how to better recognize social situations. It can also improve the patient’s memory, attention span, cognitive flexibility, and ability to organize his or her thoughts. It combines computer-based brain training and group sessions.

Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)

Interpersonal psychotherapy (IPT) was initially created as a therapy to treat depression (Major Depressive Disorder), but has since been modified to treat other mental disorders like Anxiety Disorder (particularly generalized anxiety disorder and social phobia disorders), Bipolar and Related Disorders, Substance-Related and Addictive Disorders, and Dysthymia. IPT focuses on understanding the personal relationship issues that the patient is experiencing and address those issues.

Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)

The patient is put under anesthesia in electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), and small electrical impulses are transmitted through the brain to intentionally cause a brief seizure. These currents stimulate neurons that are dormant as a result of depression or other mental illnesses. ECT is generally used when other treatments like medication and therapy are unsuccessful. While ECT has previously been associated with memory loss and other side effects, current practices are much safer and rarely pose negative side effects.

Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS)

Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS) requires an implanted device under the skin of a patient’s chest. This device will send electrical signals through the vagus nerve into the brain. Patients are numbed through medication (awake) or put under anesthesia (unconscious) for the duration of procedure. The benefits of VNS on depression are still mixed, and should be used generally be a last resort after doctor consultation, medication, therapy, and ECT.

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is similar to ECT except that it is noninvasive and does not require anesthesia.In TMS, an electromagnetic coil is placed against the patient's scalp and provides repetitive magnetic pulses which stimulate the mood-controlling nerve cells in the brain. These pulses activate neurons that have become inactive due to depression or other illnesses.

Psychodynamic Therapy

Psychodynamic therapy focuses on a patient’s unconscious thoughts and how those thoughts are manifesting in outward behavior. the therapist helps the client understand the underlying reasons behind their behavior. The theory is that bringing these unconscious patterns of thought to the surface will help the patient start to cope. Psychodynamic therapy has been used to treat Depressive Disorders, Anxiety Disorders, PTSD, and some types of Bipolar and Related Disorders.
